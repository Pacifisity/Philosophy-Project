Metaphysics: The Problem of Free Will

Often indoctrination is related to blind belief. Being able to question those beliefs is used to split apart education and indoctrination. However, in the context of free it can be seen as a form of indoctrination of knowledge when accepted blindly. If someone believes that all human behavior is determined, then wouldn’t they never question free will? It’s a form of indoctrination because it creates an illusion that questioning certain behaviors is all determined. It creates a sort of loop, if I can’t think of anything myself then how can I be free and if I’m not free how can I question my beliefs? This doesn’t recognize compatibilism explicitly at all, although it uses the analogy of frustrators that can make choices within the system but ultimately still says that those choices were determined. The video was simplified and was missing many thoughts on free will.

Two Imperatives in Education: Change of Culture v. Transmission of the Status Quo

This article discusses the ongoing debate over changes made to their education system. Specifically on conflicts between changes for new thinking and addressing social issues and the other for traditional values and culture. Both believe that their view is correct and therefore would one side winning be considered a type of indoctrination. I believe that it will allow people to explore indoctrination not only as a way of rigid thinking, but also as a way of belief. There will always be discussion on right and wrong ways to do something and often it will never be clear cut but believing that there’s not a clear answer is also a rigid belief. Indoctrination isn’t as simple as finding a balance or choosing a side, it’s just about what works for you and never taking 100% of what someone says to be factual. The author seems to have some bias towards preservation of the current traditions. Often when talking about modern topics they will overgeneralize and give poor description of their terms. However, if you keep that in mind while reading then it will allow you to recognize subtle indoctrination and the lack of information, they give you to think critically and can also be a kind of exploration.

Philosophy Now. “Two Imperatives in Education: Change of Culture V. Transmission of the Status Quo | Issue 9 | Philosophy Now.” Philosophy Now, philosophynow.org/issues/9/Two_Imperatives_in_Education_change_of_culture_v_transmission_of_the_status_quo.

Western Philosophy, an Anthology: Third Edition

Socrates argues that knowledge isn’t just correct opinions but being able to put those ideas into practice. This practice Socrates refers to can be considered the questioning part of indoctrination that many aren’t quite clear of. Socrates suggests that with his definition of “true knowledge” that education can still be considered indoctrination if the students are not allowed to put it into practice and make their own conclusions. They allow readers to make a distinction between objective truth and opinion. Often knowledge is subjective and/or relative based on different people’s perspectives. It is very easy to start a discussion on what knowledge or application are and if those are subjective. Especially in specific cultural contexts of how they describe knowledge.

Plato. "Knowledge versus Opinion." Cottingham, John. Western Philosophy an Anthology: Third Edition. John Wiley & Sons Ltd, 2021. 3-11. Book.

My Position

Indoctrination can be both a good and bad thing. We often receive the word in a negative connotation but some things don't benefit from thoroughly picking at every loose end. It's easiest to call it a bad thing when relating it to ideologies with very little evidence for or against either side. I believe this is because people want to hide their lack of evidence when trying to discuss their views.
