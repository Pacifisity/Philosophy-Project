Connection

W. K. Clifford

Indoctrination is closely linked with education, but more specifically; it’s taught “beliefs” or “ideologies.” Along the lines of belief, Clifford mentions something that resembles indoctrination and adds a moral right or wrong to the mix. He says “The question of right or wrong has to do with the origin of his belief, not the matter of it; not what it was, but how he got it; not whether it turned out to be true or false, but whether he had a right to believe on such evidence as was before him.” He uses this in reference to a boat that is not quite ready to go out to sea but he chose to let it set sail anyway because it’s worked every time before. This same thinking can lead people to relinquish critical analysis of their own beliefs. Sure, this specific way of thinking has worked before but what about when it doesn’t? Clifford suggests that it doesn’t matter whether what you were taught is right or wrong, but whether you have “insufficient evidence.”

al-Ghazālī’s Dream Argument for Skepticism

In the article of al-ghazali's dream argument it talks about skepticism. Specifically, it talks about al-ghazali’s journey through skepticism and questioning his beliefs. One analogy he uses that correlates with indoctrination is the blindness of our sight. He says “Sight also looks at a star and sees it as something small, the size of a dinar: then geometrical proofs demonstrate that it surpasses the earth in size.” Without our judgment, we would believe the sun to be the same size as a baseball. However, he also mentions a stance on absolutely certainty and doubt free knowledge. The definition he gives is “that in which the thing known is made so manifest that no doubt clings to it, nor is it accompanied by the possibility of error and deception, nor can the mind even suppose such a possibility.” Indoctrination is often attributed to a lack of critical thinking about knowledge because you were taught to have a firm belief and ignore any doubt. What distinctions can be made between knowledge that can’t be doubted and knowledge that you don’t doubt?

My Position

Thoughts

Logically, I agree with Clifford’s thoughts on “reasonable doubt.” Indoctrination can be both good and bad depending on certain scenarios. However, if you have a reasonable amount of doubt about a subject I believe it is only right for you to think critically about your ideologies. That same line of thinking goes into what I think about al-ghazali’s thoughts on judgment. The sun being the size of a baseball is a ridiculous example, but things that we find so obvious now could be the exact reason we refuse to think critically about our beliefs. Why do we know it’s not the size of a baseball? We need to keep thinking deeply, critically and reason with our views. The moment you say “it’s obvious” is the moment you blind yourself to new senses. There should be a limit though, I don’t want to teach someone about what a “string” is in a programming language and then they constantly ask me “why.” This would hinder the process of me sharing my knowledge and understanding with someone and also irritate me.

Counter

If your student is asking questions, at what point can you say they are beyond “reasonable doubt?” Every student is different and may need that information to further reinforce their understanding. What you might believe is basic knowledge may just be that bit of information that your student is lacking.

Explanation

It’s true, no matter how much time I spend with a student I will never be able to establish a perfect boundary. Therefore, I believe that everyone involved should think critically about each of their views. This will allow us to dynamically keep ourselves in touch with those around us and gauge how much support a student needs. Relating to education, indoctrination is most often related to ideologies and politics. I believe it’s the teacher’s responsibility to give the student as much knowledge as possible about every side so that way they can create their own understanding without the teacher as a guide. Also, there are more physical constraints that relate to education. Sometimes, you don’t have the time and resources that the child needs. While different students have varying needs, ultimately you can only do your best.
